  • Vol regime: elevated and transitioning higher, not compressing. VIX at 25.09 is above the 20 threshold that historically separates "normal" from "risk-off" regimes. More importantly, VVIX at 126.50 — up 14.43% — is accelerating faster than VIX itself. This is the signature of a market that is buying vol-of-vol protection, meaning institutional players are hedging against a VIX spike to 35–40+, not just a modest uptick. Do not fade this signal.
  • Vol is rich vs. realized — but that doesn't mean sell it. In a geopolitical shock regime, implied vol can stay rich for extended periods as the event risk premium is genuine and ongoing. The Hormuz closure is not a one-day event; it's a persistent supply disruption with binary escalation risk. Selling vol here is picking up nickels in front of a potential freight train. The correct posture is to be a selective vol buyer on dips, not a systematic seller.
  • Backwardation in the VIX term structure is the most actionable signal. When near-term VIX futures trade above longer-dated contracts, the market is pricing acute near-term risk. This structure typically resolves one of two ways: the risk event passes and the curve snaps back to contango (vol crush), or the event escalates and the entire curve shifts higher. Given the Hormuz situation, the escalation path has a non-trivial probability. Calendar spreads (long front-month, short back-month VIX) are expensive here — the carry is negative.
  • SKEW at 145.04 confirms the tail-risk hedging bid is structural, not tactical. SKEW measures the relative cost of OTM puts vs. ATM options. At 145, the market is paying a significant premium for downside protection. This level of skew is consistent with institutional portfolios actively buying crash protection — not a sign of complacency. The implication: if equities do sell off sharply, the put hedges will amplify dealer gamma-hedging flows (dealers short puts, forced to sell futures as market falls), creating a potential acceleration dynamic.
  • Key gamma flip level to watch: SPX 6,550–6,600. Based on the current vol surface and the concentration of open interest, dealer gamma exposure likely flips from positive to negative (from stabilizing to destabilizing) in the 6,550–6,600 zone. Below that level, dealer hedging flows become pro-cyclical — selling into weakness — which can accelerate moves. The ES futures bounce to 6,740 pre-market puts us above this zone for now, but a weak open that fails to hold 6,600 cash would be a significant technical and structural warning
